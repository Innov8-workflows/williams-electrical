When does your consumer unit need replacing?

A consumer unit (often called a fuse board or fuse box) is the heart of your home's electrics. It distributes power to every circuit and, critically, shuts circuits off automatically if something goes wrong. The trouble is that millions of UK homes still have units that were fitted before modern safety standards came in.

Common signs your fuse board is due for an upgrade:

  • Wooden-backed or metal-fronted unit — pre-2008 designs no longer meet current regulations.
  • Rewireable fuses (the screw-in or pull-out kind) — replaced industry-wide by RCBOs and MCBs.
  • No RCD protection on socket or lighting circuits — required since the 17th Edition came into force.
  • Burn marks, scorching, or a burning smell around the unit — stop using it and call us immediately.
  • Circuits tripping repeatedly — symptom of overload, fault, or end-of-life equipment.
  • Selling, letting, or remortgaging — most surveyors flag pre-2008 units in their report.

What's involved in a consumer unit upgrade

A standard domestic upgrade takes 4–6 hours. We isolate your main supply, remove the old unit, install a new dual-RCD or full-RCBO consumer unit (your choice — we'll explain the difference and quote both), reconnect every circuit, and then test the whole installation against BS 7671.

Every upgrade includes:

  • Surge protection device (SPD) to BS EN 61643 — protects appliances from lightning and grid surges.
  • Individual circuit testing — insulation resistance, earth continuity, polarity, RCD trip times.
  • A signed Electrical Installation Certificate (EIC) issued the same day.
  • Part P notification to your local authority via NICEIC — you'll receive a certificate of compliance through the post.
  • A clear-down clean — we sheet up, hoover up, and leave the cupboard tidier than we found it.

Dual-RCD vs. RCBO board — which is right?

A dual-RCD board splits your circuits between two main RCDs. It's the more common, lower-cost option. The trade-off: if one RCD trips, every circuit on that side loses power.

A full-RCBO board gives every circuit its own individual RCD + MCB combined unit. If one circuit faults, only that circuit drops out — the rest of the house stays on. It costs a bit more but it's the better long-term choice for properties with EV chargers, home offices, or anything you really don't want losing power.

What does a consumer unit upgrade cost?

Most domestic upgrades fall in the £550–£950 range for a dual-RCD installation, £750–£1,250 for full-RCBO. Final cost depends on:

  • The size of the unit (number of ways/circuits).
  • The condition of the existing wiring (anything we find that needs remedial work will be flagged and quoted before we start).
  • Whether the existing position is still suitable, or whether we need to relocate the unit to meet height/access requirements.

We always quote in writing before work starts. No call-out fee for quotes.
